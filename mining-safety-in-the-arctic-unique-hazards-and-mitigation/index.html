Mining Safety in the Arctic: Unique Hazards and Mitigation

By Nina Mortensen · 2026-03-23

mining safetyarctichazardsrisk management

Arctic mining operations face safety challenges that go well beyond those encountered in temperate-zone mining. The extreme cold, remote location, and limited emergency response capability create a unique risk profile that requires specialized safety systems and protocols. This article examines the key hazards and mitigation strategies.

Cold-Related Hazards

Frostbite and hypothermia:

  • Temperatures at Skaergaard can reach -40°C or lower during winter
  • Exposed skin can suffer frostbite in minutes at these temperatures
  • Hypothermia risk during equipment breakdown or emergency situations
  • Mitigation: Heated vehicles and cabins, mandatory cold-weather PPE, buddy system, emergency shelters at regular intervals, strict exposure time limits

    • Cold stress on equipment:

  • Steel becomes brittle, increasing risk of structural failure
  • Hydraulic and electrical systems can fail in extreme cold
  • Fuel and lubricant performance degrades
  • Mitigation: Arctic-specification equipment, heated storage facilities, cold-weather maintenance protocols, redundant systems

    • Ice and snow:

  • Slippery surfaces create fall hazards
  • Ice accumulation on structures creates overhead hazards
  • Snow loading can collapse buildings and infrastructure
  • Mitigation: Regular de-icing, snow removal programs, structural design for snow loads, non-slip surfaces

    • Remote Location Hazards

    Limited emergency response:

  • Nearest hospital may be hours away by air
  • Weather conditions may prevent medical evacuation for days
  • Search and rescue capability is limited
  • Mitigation: On-site medical clinic with advanced capabilities, emergency medical evacuation protocols, satellite communication, trained emergency responders on-site, regular emergency drills

    • Communication challenges:

  • Satellite communication can be affected by weather and solar activity
  • No cellular coverage in remote areas
  • Mitigation: Redundant communication systems (satellite phone, VSAT, radio), emergency communication protocols

    • Supply chain vulnerability:

  • Critical supplies (spare parts, fuel, medical) may not be available locally
  • Shipping disruptions due to weather or ice can interrupt supply
  • Mitigation: 6-12 month inventory of critical spare parts, fuel storage capacity, emergency supply protocols

    • Environmental Hazards

    Polar bears:

  • Polar bears are present in parts of East Greenland and can pose a threat to workers
  • Mitigation: Bear awareness training, bear monitors, deterrents (flare guns, noise makers), secure camps with bear-proof fencing, armed bear guards if necessary

    • Glacial hazards:

  • Glacier calving, crevasse falls, and glacial flooding (jokulhlaup) can occur near glaciers
  • Mitigation: Glacial hazard assessments, exclusion zones near glaciers, monitoring systems

    • Permafrost instability:

  • Ground subsidence from permafrost thaw can damage infrastructure and create collapse hazards
  • Mitigation: Permafrost monitoring, thermosiphon installation, foundation design for thaw conditions

    • Aviation Hazards

    Helicopter and fixed-wing operations:

  • Arctic flying conditions are challenging due to weather, icing, and limited visibility
  • Whiteout conditions can make landing extremely hazardous
  • Mitigation: Minimum weather standards for flight operations, experienced Arctic pilots, instrument flight capability, alternative landing sites

    • Fire and Explosion

    Fire risk in cold climates:

  • Fire risk is paradoxically higher in cold climates due to heating systems, fuel storage, and electrical systems
  • Fire suppression systems can freeze
  • Water for fire fighting may freeze
  • Mitigation: Fire detection and suppression systems rated for Arctic conditions, foam-based fire suppression, heated fire water systems, regular fire drills

    • Ergonomic and Fatigue Hazards

    Physical demands:

  • Working in cold weather is physically demanding
  • Heavy PPE restricts movement and reduces dexterity
  • Long shifts and remote camp conditions can cause fatigue
  • Mitigation: Fitness-for-work assessments, ergonomic workstation design, reasonable shift lengths (12-hour maximum), mandatory rest periods

    • Fatigue management**:

  • FIFO rotations create fatigue during travel and acclimatization
  • 24-hour daylight in summer can disrupt sleep patterns
  • Social isolation can affect mental health
  • Mitigation: Adequate rest facilities, fatigue risk management systems, mental health support, family communication access

    • Safety Performance in Arctic Mining

    Arctic mining operations can achieve strong safety records:

  • Agnico Eagle's Nunavut operations (Meliadine, Meadowbank) have achieved strong safety metrics through Arctic-specific safety programs
  • Boliden's Arctic operations in Sweden have developed成熟的cold-weather safety systems
  • Key success factors: experienced Arctic workforce, strong safety culture, adequate investment in safety systems, and continuous improvement

    • Skaergaard Safety Planning

    For Skaergaard, safety planning must address:

  • Arctic-specific hazard identification and risk assessment (HIRA)
  • Cold-weather PPE standards and policies
  • Emergency response planning for remote location
  • Polar bear management plan
  • Aviation safety protocols for helicopter and fixed-wing operations
  • Fire prevention and suppression systems
  • Fatigue risk management for FIFO operations
  • Ongoing safety training and drill programs

    • Conclusion

    Arctic mining safety requires specialized planning, systems, and training beyond what is needed in temperate-zone mining. The remote location, extreme cold, and limited emergency response capability create unique hazards that must be proactively managed. For Skaergaard, a comprehensive Arctic safety program is not optional. It is a fundamental requirement for operational success, regulatory compliance, and social license. The investment in Arctic safety systems and culture will pay dividends in reduced incidents, improved productivity, and community trust.
