Water Treatment at Arctic Mine Sites: Challenges and Technologies

By Nina Mortensen · 2026-03-23

water treatmentarctic miningenvironmentalwater quality

Water management is a central environmental challenge for Arctic mining. The pristine nature of Arctic water systems, the sensitivity of aquatic ecosystems, and the challenges of operating treatment systems in extreme cold create a unique set of requirements. This article examines how Arctic mining operations manage water quality.

Why Water Management Matters

Arctic water systems are particularly sensitive to contamination:

  • Low temperatures: Cold water temperatures slow natural degradation of contaminants. Biological treatment processes are less effective
  • Low nutrient levels: Arctic waters are typically oligotrophic (nutrient-poor), meaning even small contamination inputs can have significant effects
  • Long food chains: Arctic aquatic food chains are long and complex, meaning contaminants can bioaccumulate through multiple trophic levels
  • Cultural importance: Freshwater and marine systems are central to Inuit culture, subsistence hunting, and food security
  • Slow recovery: Arctic ecosystems recover from contamination more slowly than temperate ecosystems

Water Sources and Uses

Mining operations require water for:

  • Processing: Ore grinding, flotation, and concentrate handling use significant volumes of water
  • Dust suppression: Water is sprayed on haul roads and ore handling areas to control dust
  • Camp use: Potable water for the accommodation camp, cooking, and sanitation
  • Fire suppression: Emergency fire suppression systems require water reserves
  • Equipment cooling: Processing equipment and power generation may require cooling water

Water Sources

  • Surface water: Rivers, lakes, and streams near the mine site. The most common source but subject to seasonal flow variations
  • Groundwater: Wells drilled into aquifers. More consistent flow but may be limited in permafrost regions
  • Meltwater: Snow and ice melt during summer provides seasonal water. Useful as supplemental source
  • Desalination: Seawater desalination is energy-intensive but provides a reliable source in coastal locations

Water Treatment Technologies

Settling and clarification:

  • Simple gravity settling removes suspended solids
  • Effective for tailings water and general mine runoff
  • May require heating to prevent freezing in Arctic conditions

Chemical treatment:

  • Coagulation and flocculation using chemical agents to remove fine suspended solids
  • pH adjustment using lime or other agents
  • Oxidation for removal of specific contaminants (cyanide, ammonia)
  • Chemical consumption adds operating cost

Biological treatment:

  • Biological processes (activated sludge, biofilters) degrade organic contaminants
  • Less effective in cold water (microbial activity slows at low temperatures)
  • Solutions: Heated bioreactors, cold-adapted microbial cultures

Membrane filtration:

  • Reverse osmosis and ultrafiltration for advanced water treatment
  • Effective for dissolved metals and other dissolved contaminants
  • Energy-intensive and sensitive to fouling
  • Membrane systems must be protected from freezing

Constructed wetlands:

  • Engineered wetland systems for passive water treatment
  • Low operating cost but large land area required
  • Limited effectiveness in Arctic conditions due to short growing season and winter freezing
  • May be suitable for post-closure passive treatment

Tailings Water Management

Tailings water (process water from ore processing) requires specific management:

  • Water recycling: Recycling process water within the processing circuit minimizes fresh water demand and discharge
  • Decant management: Water that accumulates on top of tailings storage must be managed carefully to prevent overflow or seepage
  • Seepage collection: Systems to collect and treat any water that seeps through containment structures
  • Zero discharge: Best practice for Arctic operations. All water is recycled within the system with no discharge to the environment

Stormwater Management

Arctic stormwater has unique characteristics:

  • Spring breakup: Rapid snowmelt generates high stormwater flows in a short period
  • Frozen ground: Ground may still be frozen during spring breakup, reducing infiltration and increasing runoff
  • Contaminant mobilization: Spring stormwater can mobilize contaminants from exposed surfaces and stockpiles
  • Winter: Minimal stormwater flow during frozen conditions, but any discharge must be managed

Management: Containment basins, diversion channels, and treatment systems designed for peak spring flows.

Wastewater Treatment

Camp wastewater (sewage, kitchen, shower water) requires treatment:

  • Package treatment plants: Standard package wastewater treatment systems adapted for cold weather
  • Heated systems: Treatment tanks and pipes must be heated to prevent freezing
  • Effluent standards: Treated wastewater must meet Greenland's environmental standards before discharge

Monitoring and Compliance

Arctic water quality monitoring requires:

  • Year-round monitoring: Water quality must be monitored throughout the year, including winter when access is difficult
  • Automated stations: Remote monitoring stations with telemetry provide continuous data
  • Ice thickness monitoring: Monitoring of ice thickness on tailings ponds and water bodies
  • Biological monitoring: Regular sampling of aquatic organisms for contaminant bioaccumulation

Skaergaard Water Management

For Skaergaard, water management will be a significant environmental and cost consideration:

  • Water source: Likely surface water from local streams or meltwater, supplemented by groundwater wells
  • Processing water: Closed-circuit recycling system to minimize fresh water demand
  • Tailings water: Zero-discharge system with all process water recycled within the circuit
  • Camp wastewater: Heated package treatment plant meeting Greenland standards
  • Stormwater: Collection and treatment system designed for spring breakup peak flows
  • Monitoring: Year-round automated water quality monitoring network

Conclusion

Water management in Arctic mining requires specialized technologies, systems, and monitoring to protect some of the world's most sensitive aquatic ecosystems. The challenges of cold temperatures, seasonal variations, and remote location add cost and complexity. For Skaergaard, a zero-discharge water management approach with comprehensive recycling and monitoring is the standard that should be targeted. The investment in water management systems and monitoring will be essential for environmental compliance and maintaining social license.
