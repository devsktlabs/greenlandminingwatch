Why Due Diligence Matters in Arctic Mining Investments

By Nina Mortensen · 2026-03-23

due diligencearctic mininginvestmentrisk assessment

Due diligence is the process of verifying information, assessing risk, and confirming the investment thesis before committing capital. For Arctic mining investments, due diligence requires attention to factors that may not arise in more established mining jurisdictions. This article provides a practical framework.

Key Due Diligence Areas

1. Geology and Resources

  • Verify the resource estimate against drill data and QA/QC results
  • Assess geological risk (grade continuity, geological complexity)
  • Review the qualified person's track record and independence
  • Understand the resource classification and what it means for project confidence

    • 2. Metallurgy and Processing

  • Review metallurgical test work results
  • Assess recovery assumptions for each metal (Pd, Au, Pt, Rh)
  • Evaluate the processing flow sheet for Arctic conditions
  • Confirm concentrate specifications meet smelter requirements

    • 3. Mining and Infrastructure

  • Review the mine plan and production schedule
  • Assess infrastructure requirements and costs
  • Evaluate Arctic-specific design considerations (permafrost, heating, access)
  • Confirm logistics plan (shipping, concentrate storage, supply chain)

    • 4. Environmental and Social

  • Review the EIA status and environmental baseline data
  • Assess community engagement and support
  • Evaluate environmental risks and mitigation measures
  • Confirm financial assurance for closure and reclamation

    • 5. Regulatory and Political

  • Review the permitting timeline and status
  • Assess regulatory risk (policy changes, political opposition)
  • Evaluate jurisdictional quality (legal framework, governance, stability)
  • Understand the fiscal regime (royalties, taxes, incentives)

    • 6. Financial

  • Review the financial model and key assumptions
  • Assess sensitivity to metal prices, costs, and production rate
  • Evaluate financing plan (equity, debt, royalty, government support)
  • Confirm capital and operating cost estimates against comparable projects

    • 7. Management

  • Assess the management team's experience with Arctic mining
  • Review the company's track record and governance
  • Evaluate the advisory board and technical consultants
  • Confirm succession planning for key roles

    • Arctic-Specific Due Diligence

    Several factors require additional attention in Arctic mining due diligence:

  • **Permafrost**: Is the geotechnical characterization of permafrost adequate? Are foundation designs appropriate for warming permafrost?
  • **Climate change**: Has the project design accounted for projected climate change over the mine life and post-closure?
  • **Seasonal constraints**: Are construction and shipping schedules realistic given Arctic conditions?
  • **Remote logistics**: Is the logistics plan robust enough for year-round operations?
  • **Emergency response**: Are emergency response plans adequate for remote Arctic locations?
  • **Indigenous rights**: Have FPIC principles been followed? Are community agreements in place?

    • Common Due Diligence Pitfalls

  • Over-reliance on management projections without independent verification
  • Insufficient attention to QA/QC in resource estimation
  • Underestimating Arctic cost premiums (labor, energy, logistics)
  • Assuming permitting timelines will be met without contingency
  • Neglecting social and environmental risk factors
  • Failing to conduct a site visit

    • Information Sources

  • **Technical reports**: NI 43-101, JORC, or PERC technical reports
  • **Corporate filings**: SEC filings (for US-listed companies), annual reports, MD&A
  • **Government records**: Permit applications, environmental assessments, regulatory correspondence
  • **Independent consultants**: Engaging independent technical, legal, and environmental advisors
  • **Site visits**: Direct observation of the project, community, and management

    • Conclusion

    Due diligence on Arctic mining investments requires going beyond standard mining due diligence to address Arctic-specific risks including permafrost, climate change, remote logistics, and indigenous rights. A thorough due diligence process protects investors from unpleasant surprises and provides confidence in the investment thesis. For Skaergaard, due diligence should focus on resource confidence, metallurgical performance, Arctic-specific cost assumptions, environmental risk, and management capability.
