The Hidden Costs of Arctic Mining: What feasibility Studies Often Miss

By Nina Mortensen · 2026-03-23

arctic mininghidden costsfeasibilityriskcapex

Feasibility studies for Arctic mining projects face unique challenges. Certain cost categories are routinely underestimated or overlooked, creating cost overruns that erode project economics. This article highlights the hidden costs that investors should scrutinize.

Infrastructure Mobilization

Getting people and equipment to a remote Arctic site costs more than most studies project:

  • **Heavy equipment transport**: Shipping mining trucks, excavators, and processing equipment to a remote Arctic site requires specialized vessels, port facilities, and overland transport. Costs are 50-100% higher than in established mining regions
  • **Construction camp mobilization**: Transporting and assembling a 300-person mining camp in the Arctic is a major logistical exercise
  • **Fuel logistics**: Transporting and storing 6-12 months of diesel fuel at a remote site requires large tank farms and marine logistics

    • Weather Delays

    Weather is the single largest source of schedule and cost overrun in Arctic mining:

  • **Construction delays**: Storms, blizzards, and extreme cold can halt construction for days or weeks. Feasibility studies often assume optimal weather
  • **Shipping delays**: Ice conditions or storms can delay concentrate shipment windows, requiring additional storage capacity
  • **Drilling delays**: Exploration and resource drilling programs are frequently delayed by weather
  • **Hidden cost**: Weather delays typically add 10-20% to construction timelines and 5-15% to construction costs

    • Energy Costs

    Feasibility studies often underestimate energy costs:

  • **Diesel price volatility**: Arctic mines are particularly exposed to diesel price fluctuations because fuel represents a large portion of operating costs
  • **Heating loads**: The energy required to heat camps, processing plants, and infrastructure in extreme cold is often underestimated
  • **Power generation redundancy**: Arctic power systems require more redundancy (backup generators, heated fuel systems) than temperate-zone operations

    • Environmental Monitoring and Compliance

    Environmental costs are frequently underestimated:

  • **Year-round monitoring**: Arctic environmental monitoring is more expensive due to remote access and extreme conditions
  • **Permit compliance**: First-in-jurisdiction projects may face additional regulatory requirements that are not anticipated
  • **Closure and post-closure**: Closure costs for Arctic mines are higher than for temperate-zone mines due to permafrost stability requirements and longer monitoring periods

    • Workforce Turnover

    Arctic mining operations face higher workforce turnover:

  • **Remote rotation work**: FIFO workers may not tolerate Arctic conditions long-term
  • **Training costs**: Higher turnover means higher ongoing training costs
  • **Recruitment costs**: Recruiting workers for remote Arctic locations is expensive and time-consuming
  • **Productivity loss**: New workers are less productive than experienced workers during the learning curve

    • Community and Social Costs

    Community-related costs are often underestimated:

  • **Impact Benefit Agreements**: The cost of IBAs (employment programs, training, infrastructure, revenue sharing) may increase as negotiations progress
  • **Community infrastructure**: Mining companies may be expected to invest in community infrastructure (housing, schools, health facilities) beyond what is legally required
  • **Cultural accommodation**: Accommodating traditional hunting, fishing, and cultural practices may restrict operations or require additional infrastructure

    • Contingency Adequacy

    Feasibility studies include contingency allowances, but these are often insufficient for Arctic projects:

  • **Standard contingency**: 10-15% of capital costs is typical for feasibility studies
  • **Arctic contingency**: Arctic projects should carry 20-30% contingency due to higher uncertainty
  • **Many studies use standard contingency**: This is a systematic underestimation of Arctic project risk

    • What Investors Should Do

  • Review the contingency assumptions: Is the contingency adequate for an Arctic project?
  • Assess the weather delay allowance: Has realistic weather downtime been included?
  • Scrutinize energy costs: Are heating loads and fuel price volatility adequately modeled?
  • Evaluate community cost assumptions: Are IBAs and community investment commitments realistic?
  • Compare to comparable Arctic projects: How do costs compare to Agnico Eagle's Nunavut operations or similar Arctic mines?

    • Conclusion

    Arctic mining projects face cost categories that are systematically underestimated in feasibility studies. Weather delays, energy costs, mobilization logistics, environmental compliance, and community obligations all carry higher costs than in temperate-zone mining. For investors, scrutinizing these hidden cost categories and ensuring adequate contingency is essential for realistic project economics.
