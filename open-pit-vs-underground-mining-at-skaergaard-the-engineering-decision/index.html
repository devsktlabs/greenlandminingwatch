Open-Pit vs Underground Mining at Skaergaard: The Engineering Decision

By Nina Mortensen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardopen pitundergroundmining methodengineering

The choice between open-pit and underground mining is one of the most consequential engineering decisions for any mining project. For Skaergaard, this decision affects capital cost, operating cost, production rate, environmental impact, and mine life. This article examines the trade-offs.

Open-Pit Mining

Open-pit mining involves excavating the ore body from the surface in progressively deeper benches.

Advantages for Skaergaard:

  • Lower unit mining cost per tonne of ore (US$3-5/t vs US$8-15/t for underground)
  • Higher production rates (potentially 5-10 million tonnes per year)
  • Simpler geotechnical environment (no underground stability concerns)
  • Better ore control and selectivity at surface
  • Easier exploration of extensions at depth from surface

    • Disadvantages for Skaergaard:

  • Larger surface disturbance and environmental impact
  • Higher stripping ratio at depth (more waste rock per tonne of ore)
  • Limited by pit depth (typically 300-500 meters practical limit)
  • Higher capital cost for mobile fleet
  • Greater water management challenges from larger disturbed area

    • Economic limit: Open-pit mining is economic as long as the revenue from the ore exceeds the cost of mining it, including the cost of moving waste rock (stripping). The stripping ratio (waste:ore) is the key metric.

    Underground Mining

    Underground mining involves accessing the ore body through shafts, ramps, or declines and extracting ore from within the deposit.

    Advantages for Skaergaard:

  • Can access ore below the economic limit of open-pit mining
  • Smaller surface disturbance and environmental impact
  • Potentially higher ore grades at depth (grade may increase with depth in layered intrusions)
  • Longer mine life from accessing deeper ore

    • Disadvantages for Skaergaard:

  • Higher unit mining cost (US$8-15/t)
  • Lower production rates (typically 1-3 million tonnes per year)
  • Greater geotechnical complexity (ground support, ventilation, rock mechanics)
  • Higher development capital (shafts, ramps, underground infrastructure)
  • More challenging Arctic conditions underground (ventilation heating, permafrost)

    • The Typical Sequence: Open-Pit First, Underground Later

    Most large deposits are mined in two phases:

  • **Open-pit phase**: 10-15 years of open-pit mining extracting near-surface ore
  • **Underground phase**: Additional 10-15 years of underground mining below the open-pit

    • This approach maximizes early cash flow from lower-cost open-pit mining while preserving the option for underground extension.

    Skaergaard-Specific Considerations

    Geological setting: Skaergaard is a layered intrusion where PGM mineralization occurs in specific horizons within the intrusion. The mineralized layers are relatively flat-lying and extend across a large area, which is favorable for open-pit mining.

    Depth to mineralization: The primary PGM and gold mineralization at Skaergaard occurs at relatively shallow depths, within reach of open-pit mining.

    Strip ratio: The strip ratio (waste:ore) increases with pit depth. The economic limit for open-pit mining will be determined by the point where the strip ratio makes open-pit mining uneconomic relative to underground mining.

    Grade continuity: The mineralized layers at Skaergaard show reasonable grade continuity, which is favorable for both open-pit and underground mining.

    Permafrost: Underground mining in permafrost presents additional challenges (ground freezing, ice in excavations, ventilation heating). Open-pit mining avoids most of these challenges.

    Economic Comparison

    Open-pit only (assuming 10-15 year mine life):

  • Lower unit mining cost
  • Higher production rate
  • Higher NPV over a shorter time frame
  • Less total metal recovered (ore below pit limit left in ground)

    • Open-pit + underground (assuming 20-30 year mine life):

  • Lower overall unit cost (open-pit ore subsidizes underground development)
  • Longer mine life
  • More total metal recovered
  • Higher total capital cost
  • Higher total NPV despite lower average return

    • Environmental Trade-offs

    Open-pit advantages: Simpler closure, more predictable environmental impact, easier reclamation.

    Open-pit disadvantages: Larger surface disturbance, more water management, visual impact.

    Underground advantages: Smaller surface footprint, less visual impact, lower long-term environmental risk.

    Underground disadvantages: Potential for subsidence, groundwater impacts, more complex closure.

    What GRML Will Likely Choose

    Based on the geological setting and project economics, GRML will likely adopt the conventional approach:

    GRML is led by Joseph Sinkule (Founder, CEO, Director, and Chairman).

  • **Phase 1 (Years 1-12)**: Open-pit mining of near-surface mineralized horizons
  • **Phase 2 (Years 12-25+)**: Underground mining accessing deeper extensions of the same mineralized horizons

    • This approach provides the best balance of early cash flow, total metal recovery, and NPV.

    Conclusion

    The mining method decision for Skaergaard will follow the conventional sequence of open-pit mining followed by underground extension. Open-pit mining provides lower costs and higher early production, while underground mining extends the mine life and recovers additional ore below the pit limit. For investors, the mining method decision will be formalized in the feasibility study and will be a key determinant of project economics. The favorable geological setting at Skaergaard supports both mining methods.
